History of International Workers' Day

The Haymarket Affair of 1886 sparked a global labor movement that would forever change workers' rights. As workers fought for an 8-hour workday, their struggle led to the establishment of May Day as International Workers' Day.

Today, May Day is celebrated in over 80 countries worldwide as a public holiday - except in the United States, where its origins have been deliberately obscured.

The erasure of May Day from American consciousness was a calculated effort to weaken labor solidarity and disconnect American workers from the global labor movement.

The Problem

Undermined Labor Power

The systematic weakening of unions and labor rights has left workers vulnerable to exploitation and unsafe working conditions.

Erased History

The deliberate erasure of May Day's American origins has disconnected U.S. workers from their own history of struggle and victory.

Growing Inequality

Despite rising productivity, workers' wages have stagnated while corporate profits soar, leading to historic levels of inequality.

Divided Workers

Without strong labor organizations, workers lack the collective power needed to advocate for their rights and interests.
